Descent Methods

Descent methods for unconstrained optimization follow at each iteration a search direction related to the current gradient. There are a variety of methods for generating valid descent directions, including steepest descent, scaled descent, coordinate descent, and variations of Newton descent.

The methods we consider here minimize a smooth function f:Rn→Rf:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb Rf:Rn→R by generating a sequence of iterates

xk+1=xk+αkdk x_{k+1} = x_k + α_k d_k xk+1​=xk​+αk​dk​

that monotonically reduces the function value, i.e.,

f(xk+1)<f(xk)for allk∈N. f(x_{k+1}) < f(x_k) \quad\text{for all}\quad k\in\N. f(xk+1​)<f(xk​)for allk∈N.

The positive scalars αk\alpha_kαk​ are steplengths and the vectors dk∈Rnd_k\in\mathbb R^ndk​∈Rn are descent directions. Our aim is to ensure that the generated sequence converges to a stationary point:

lim⁡k∈N∥∇f(xk)∥=0. \lim_{k\in\N}\|\nabla f(x_k)\| = 0. k∈Nlim​∥∇f(xk​)∥=0.

Below we describe methods for computing the descent directions and the steplengths.

Running example

In our discussion we'll often use the Rosenbrock test function as a convenient example. Here's the function definition and its gradient and Hessian: 

using LinearAlgebra
import ForwardDiff:gradient,hessian

f(x) = (1 - x[1])^2 + 100.0(x[2] - x[1]^2)^2
∇f(x) = gradient(f, x)
∇²f(x) = hessian(f, x)

The minimizer is x∗=(1,1)x^*=(1,1)x∗=(1,1), which we verify by checking that the gradient is zero and the Hessian is positive definite at that point:

x̄ = [1., 1.]
@show norm(∇f(x̄))
@show isposdef(∇²f(x̄))
norm(∇f(x̄)) = 0.0
isposdef(∇²f(x̄)) = true

Here's a visualization of the function and the minimizer marked with a star: 

using Plots; gr()

Gradient-related directions

Observe that for any sequence of positive-definite matrices {Bk}k∈N\{B_k\}_{k\in\N}{Bk​}k∈N​, the vectors

dk=−Bk∇f(xk) d_k = -B_k\nabla f(x_k) dk​=−Bk​∇f(xk​)

are descent directions for fff at points xkx_kxk​ because the directional derivative

f′(xk;dk)=∇f(xk)Tdk=−∇f(xk)T ⁣Bk∇f(xk)<0 f'(x_k;d_k) = ∇f(x_k)^T d_k = -∇f(x_k)^T\! B_k ∇f(x_k)<0 f′(xk​;dk​)=∇f(xk​)Tdk​=−∇f(xk​)TBk​∇f(xk​)<0

whenever fff is not stationary at xkx_kxk​, i.e., ∇f(xk)≠0\nabla f(x_k)\ne0∇f(xk​)=0. This fact means that we can generate a whole family of descent directions through different choices the scaling matrices BkB_kBk​.

Stronger conditions are needed, however, to ensure that the directional derivative doesn't get arbitrarily close to zero. In particular, we need additional conditions to ensure that

lim sup⁡k∈Nf′(xk;dk)<0. \limsup_{k\in\N}f'(x_k;d_k)<0. k∈Nlimsup​f′(xk​;dk​)<0.

One approach is to require that the eigenvalues of the matrices BkB_kBk​ are uniformly positive and bounded. Thus, we assume that there exist positive constants μ1\mu_1μ1​ and μ2\mu_2μ2​ such that

0<μ1<λmin(Bk)≤λmax(Bk)<μ2∀k∈N, 0 < \mu_1 < \lambda_{\text{min}}(B_k) \le \lambda_{\text{max}}(B_k) < \mu_2 \quad \forall k\in\N, 0<μ1​<λmin​(Bk​)≤λmax​(Bk​)<μ2​∀k∈N,

where λmin(Bk)\lambda_{\text{min}}(B_k)λmin​(Bk​) and λmax(Bk)\lambda_{\text{max}}(B_k)λmax​(Bk​), respectively, are the minimum and maximum eigenvalues of BkB_kBk​. This condition implies that there are bounds on the directional gradient and the norm of the direction directly related to the gradient:

f′(xk;dk)=−∇f(xk)TBk∇f(xk)<−μ1∥∇f(xk)∥2∥dk∥=∥−Bk∇f(xk)∥≤∥Bk∥⋅∥∇f(xk)∥<μ2∥∇f(xk)∥.\begin{aligned} f'(x_k;d_k) &= -∇f(x_k)^T B_k∇f(x_k) < -μ_1∥∇f(x_k)∥^2 \\ ∥d_k∥ &= ∥{-}B_k∇f(x_k)∥≤∥B_k∥⋅∥∇f(x_k)∥<μ_2∥∇f(x_k)∥. \end{aligned}f′(xk​;dk​)∥dk​∥​=−∇f(xk​)TBk​∇f(xk​)<−μ1​∥∇f(xk​)∥2=∥−Bk​∇f(xk​)∥≤∥Bk​∥⋅∥∇f(xk​)∥<μ2​∥∇f(xk​)∥.​

Thus, the directional derivative of a gradient-related direction vanishes only when at stationary point, and the norm of a gradient-related direction is bounded by a multiple of the norm of the gradient.

Below we discuss the most-used search directions.

Steepest descent direction

The negative gradient d:=−∇f(x)d:=-∇f(x)d:=−∇f(x) is evidently a descent direction, because

f′(xk;−∇f(xk))=−∇f(xk)T∇f(xk)=−∥∇f(xk)∥2<0 f'(x_k;-∇f(x_k)) = -∇f(x_k)^T ∇f(x_k) = -\|∇f(x_k)\|^2 < 0 f′(xk​;−∇f(xk​))=−∇f(xk​)T∇f(xk​)=−∥∇f(xk​)∥2<0

whenever ∇f(xk)≠0∇f(x_k)\ne0∇f(xk​)=0. The negative gradient is often called the steepest descent direction because it's aligned with the unit-norm direction that makes the directional derivative most negative. In particular, use the Cauchy-Scharz inequality to deduce the bound

f′(xk;v)=∇f(xk)Tv≥−∥∇f(xk)∥2⋅∥v∥2. f'(x_k;v) = ∇f(x_k)^T v \ge -∥∇f(x_k)∥_2⋅∥v∥_2. f′(xk​;v)=∇f(xk​)Tv≥−∥∇f(xk​)∥2​⋅∥v∥2​.

for any direction vvv. Then observe that the direction v=−∇f(xk)v=-∇f(x_k)v=−∇f(xk​) achieves the lower bound.

The package ForwardDiff.jl implements the gradient function, which we negate and assign to steepestDescent:

steepestDescent(f, x) = -gradient(f, x)
